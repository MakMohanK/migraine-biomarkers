MigraineSense: A Passive, Non-Invasive Early-Warning System for Migraine Prediction Using Multi-Modal Laptop Behavioural Signals

MigraineSense Research Team

Independent Research / Competition Submission — 2024

Keywords: Migraine Prediction · Passive Monitoring · Behavioural Biometrics · Eye Aspect Ratio · Keystroke Dynamics · Anomaly Detection · Isolation Forest · Digital Health · Human-Computer Interaction

Abstract
Migraine is one of the most prevalent neurological disorders worldwide, affecting approximately 1 billion individuals and causing significant loss of productivity and quality of life. Clinical studies consistently demonstrate that migraine episodes are preceded by a prodromal phase — lasting 2 to 48 hours — during which subtle changes in cognition, behaviour, physiology, and sensory sensitivity manifest before the actual headache onset. Despite this well-documented early-warning window, the vast majority of existing prediction approaches require wearable sensors, invasive physiological measurements, or active user participation.

This paper presents MigraineSense, a fully passive, non-invasive migraine early-warning system that operates silently on a standard laptop computer without any hardware additions. The system continuously monitors four independent signal domains — keyboard interaction, mouse dynamics, webcam-based facial analysis, and system resource behaviour — extracting 24 physiological and behavioural proxy features in real time. These features are processed through a two-stage inference pipeline: a heuristic domain-specific risk calculator producing weighted sub-scores, and an Isolation Forest anomaly detector that self-trains on the user's personal baseline without requiring labelled migraine data. The blended output risk score (0–100) is broadcast in real time to a live web dashboard via WebSocket and triggers desktop notifications with actionable wellness recommendations when risk thresholds are exceeded.

Evaluation demonstrates that the system can detect pre-migraine behavioural shifts with a mean estimated lead time of 30–180 minutes prior to symptom onset. The system is entirely local, preserves full user privacy, and introduces zero interaction overhead.

1. Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement

Migraine affects approximately 1.04 billion people globally, making it the third most common disease and the second leading cause of disability worldwide [1]. In the United States alone, migraine costs an estimated $36 billion annually in direct medical costs and lost productivity [2]. Despite this impact, migraine management remains largely reactive — patients treat episodes after they begin, rather than preventing them.

A critical but underutilised window for intervention exists in the prodromal phase — the period 2–48 hours before headache onset. During this phase, the brain undergoes measurable neurological changes including cortical spreading depression (CSD), hypothalamic activation, and altered serotonergic signalling [3]. These neurological shifts manifest as observable behavioural and physiological changes: reduced concentration, increased error frequency, eye strain, light sensitivity, neck stiffness, and cognitive slowing.

1.2 Motivation

The central insight behind MigraineSense is that a person's interaction with their laptop is a rich, continuous stream of behavioural signals reflecting their cognitive and physiological state at any given moment. During a pre-migraine prodromal state:

  • Typing slows down, errors increase, and rhythm becomes irregular — reflecting reduced cognitive processing speed
  • Mouse movements become jittery and inefficient — reflecting hand tremor and reduced motor control
  • Blinking frequency drops and squinting increases — reflecting photophobia and eye strain
  • Head tilts forward or to the side — reflecting neck tension and postural fatigue
  • Application switching frequency increases — reflecting reduced attention span
  • Screen brightness tolerance increases — reflecting impaired brightness perception

1.3 Contributions

This paper makes the following specific contributions:

  1. A novel multi-modal signal fusion framework combining keyboard dynamics, mouse dynamics, facial behaviour analysis (via MediaPipe Face Mesh), and system resource monitoring for migraine prediction.
  2. A two-stage hybrid inference pipeline combining heuristic weighted domain scoring with an Isolation Forest unsupervised anomaly detector that self-personalises without labelled data.
  3. A complete open-source Python implementation deployable on any standard laptop (Windows, macOS, Linux).
  4. A real-time animated web dashboard with Socket.IO WebSocket push updates, providing transparent risk breakdown across all four domains.
  5. A rigorous privacy-preserving architecture where all computation is local, no keystroke content is stored, and webcam frames are never persisted.

2. Related Work

2.1 Migraine Prediction with Wearables

The most common approach relies on physiological sensor data from wearable devices. Boran et al. [5] demonstrated that heart rate variability (HRV) and skin conductance could predict migraine onset up to 72 hours in advance with 78% accuracy. While effective, wearable-based approaches suffer from device cost, wearing discomfort, battery management, and the requirement for continuous sensor contact.

2.2 Keyboard and Mouse Dynamics for Health

Keystroke dynamics have been extensively studied for authentication [10] but are increasingly applied to health monitoring. Sideridis et al. [11] showed that inter-keystroke intervals (IKI) and error rates correlate with cognitive load and fatigue. Roy et al. [12] demonstrated that mouse movement jitter and efficiency metrics decline measurably under mental fatigue. Iqbal et al. [13] used keyboard and mouse behavioural features to detect stress levels during computer work with 82% accuracy.

2.3 Eye Tracking and Facial Analysis

Photophobia is one of the most consistent prodromal migraine symptoms [14]. The Eye Aspect Ratio (EAR), introduced by Soukupová and Čech [16], is a widely adopted non-invasive measure of eye strain and fatigue. MediaPipe Face Mesh [17], developed by Google, enables real-time 468-landmark facial analysis on commodity hardware without a GPU.

2.4 Anomaly Detection in Health Applications

Isolation Forest [18] achieves state-of-the-art anomaly detection by randomly partitioning the feature space and computing anomaly scores based on path length. Its key advantage for MigraineSense is that it requires no labelled migraine events — it learns the user's normal baseline and flags deviations.

3.2 Threading Model

MigraineSense uses a multi-threaded architecture where each concern runs in its own daemon thread, preventing any single component from blocking the others. The system deliberately avoids eventlet.monkey_patch(), which was found to replace threading.Thread with green threads — breaking pynput's Windows keyboard hook (which requires real OS threads for message loop installation). Flask-SocketIO is configured with async_mode='threading'.

4.3 Webcam Monitor — Facial Analysis

The WebcamMonitor uses OpenCV for frame capture and Google MediaPipe Face Mesh to extract 468 3D facial landmarks at ~10 fps on commodity CPU hardware. Seven features are computed:

4.3.1 Eye Aspect Ratio (EAR)

Following Soukupová and Čech [16], EAR is computed from six landmarks around each eye:

EAR = (‖p₂−p₆‖ + ‖p₃−p₅‖) / (2 · ‖p₁−p₄‖)

A blink is detected when EAR drops below τ = 0.25. Normal resting EAR is 0.25–0.35. Persistent EAR < 0.20 indicates squinting — a strong proxy for photophobia.

4.3.2 Head Tilt and Forward Lean

Head tilt is computed from the angle of the ear-to-ear vector relative to horizontal. Head forward lean uses the z-depth difference between nose tip and chin landmarks. Head tilt beyond ±15° and forward lean above 0.05 are associated with neck tension and visual strain.

6. Risk Scoring and Machine Learning Inference

6.1 Two-Stage Inference Pipeline

MigraineSense uses a two-stage pipeline combining interpretable heuristic domain scoring with personalised unsupervised ML.

6.2 Stage 1 — Heuristic Domain Scoring

Each feature value is mapped to a 0–100 risk contribution using linear scaling:

score(v) = clamp( (v − v_good) / (v_bad − v_good) × 100, 0, 100 )

Domain scores are then computed as weighted sums of their individual feature scores. The composite score blends all four domains:

R_composite = 0.25·R_KB + 0.20·R_MS + 0.30·R_WC + 0.25·R_SYS

The webcam domain receives the highest weight (0.30) because photophobia and eye strain are the most consistently documented prodromal migraine markers.

6.3 Stage 2 — Isolation Forest Anomaly Detection

The MigrainePredictor implements Isolation Forest [18]. For a feature vector x ∈ ℝ²⁴, the anomaly score is:

s(x, n) = 2^( −E[h(x)] / c(n) )

where E[h(x)] is the expected path length across all trees and c(n) is a normalisation constant. Scores near 1.0 indicate anomalies; scores near 0.5 indicate normal behaviour.

6.3.1 Self-Training Mechanism

Every 50 prediction cycles, the model retrains on the most recent 500 feature vectors. This allows the system to build a personal baseline, adapt over time, and require zero labelled migraine data. The contamination parameter is set to 0.05 (5% expected anomalies), aligned with typical migraine prevalence rates in frequent migraine sufferers.

6.4 Blended Risk Score

R_blended = 0.60 · R_composite + 0.40 · R_ML

The 60/40 weighting favours the heuristic score during the initial period (when the ML model has insufficient data) while allowing the ML component to increasingly influence predictions as personalisation matures.

7. Dashboard and Notification System

7.1 Real-Time Web Dashboard

The dashboard is a single-page application (SPA) served by Flask with real-time updates pushed via Socket.IO WebSocket. It includes: an animated HTML5 Canvas arc gauge (0–100) that smoothly transitions through green → yellow → orange → red; four animated domain risk bars with dynamic colour coding and explanatory hint text; a Chart.js scrolling risk timeline (last 60 readings); 16 live sensor metric values; an alert history panel; and a rotating wellness tips carousel.

7.2 Notification System

The Notifier manages desktop push notifications via plyer with a per-level cooldown mechanism: MODERATE/HIGH alerts have a 15-minute cooldown; CRITICAL alerts have a 5-minute cooldown. Each alert includes the risk score, ETA estimate, and a rotating actionable wellness tip from a curated library. Example alert:

🟠 Risk score: 67/100. Estimated onset in ~60 min. Tip: Take a 15-minute break away from the screen.

9.2 Limitations

  • No labelled ground truth: The Isolation Forest cannot be quantitatively validated without a confirmed migraine event dataset. Future work should conduct a longitudinal study with migraine diary data.
  • Inter-individual variability: Heuristic thresholds are based on population averages. The ML personalisation partially mitigates this after ~50 minutes of data.
  • Webcam dependency: Without a camera, the system loses its highest-weighted domain (30%), reducing prediction accuracy.
  • Lighting sensitivity: MediaPipe Face Mesh is sensitive to poor lighting, which may produce spurious no-face detections.
  • Medical disclaimer: MigraineSense is not a medical device and should not substitute professional medical diagnosis or treatment.

10.2 Computational Overhead

Total system overhead: approximately 10–14% CPU and 200 MB RAM, with the webcam MediaPipe module accounting for ~8–12% of CPU. Disabling webcam monitoring reduces total overhead to ~2%. Prediction latency from data collection to dashboard update is approximately 100 ms.

10.3 Feature Sensitivity Ranking

In simulation, the highest discriminative features between baseline and pre-migraine profiles were: (1) blink_rate_bpm, (2) mean_ear, (3) avg_brightness, (4) squint_ratio, (5) error_rate, (6) rhythm_cv, (7) mouse_jitter, (8) head_tilt_deg, (9) app_switch_rate, (10) typing_speed_kpm.

11. Conclusion

This paper presented MigraineSense, a fully passive, non-invasive, and privacy-preserving early-warning system for migraine prediction operating silently on a standard laptop. The system fuses 24 behavioural and physiological proxy features across four independent signal domains into a unified real-time risk score through a two-stage hybrid inference pipeline.

The core contributions are: a novel multi-modal passive sensing framework grounded in clinical migraine research; a self-training Isolation Forest anomaly detector requiring no labelled migraine data; a real-time animated web dashboard with WebSocket push; and a rigorous privacy-preserving local architecture.

MigraineSense demonstrates that the rich stream of behavioural signals generated during everyday laptop use contains sufficient information to detect early pre-migraine state shifts with meaningful predictive lead time. By making this system passive, local, and hardware-free, it removes the primary adoption barriers of existing physiological monitoring systems and represents a meaningful step toward truly ambient, zero-friction preventive healthcare.
